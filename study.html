Semiconductor Physics & Devices

Comprehensive Notes, Derivations, and Diagrams

1. Advantages of Semiconductor over Conductor

  • Control of Conductivity: The conductivity of semiconductors can be precisely controlled by adding impurities (doping) and applying external energy (heat, light, voltage), whereas a conductor's conductivity is fixed.
  • Unidirectional Current Flow: Semiconductor devices like PN junction diodes allow current to flow in only one direction, which is impossible with standard conductors. This enables rectification.
  • Negative Temperature Coefficient: As temperature increases, the resistance of a semiconductor decreases. Conductors increase in resistance with temperature.
  • Amplification & Switching: Transistors (made of semiconductors) can amplify signals and act as digital switches, forming the basis of all modern electronics and computers.
  • Energy Efficiency & Size: Semiconductor devices consume much less power, do not require a vacuum or heating filaments (like old vacuum tubes), and can be miniaturized to nanometer scales.

2. Define Forbidden Gap (Band Gap)

The Forbidden Gap (or Energy Band Gap, $E_g$) is the energy range in a solid where no permitted electron energy states exist. It is the energy difference between the highest point of the valence band (where electrons are bound to atoms) and the lowest point of the conduction band (where electrons are free to move and conduct current).

Formula: $E_g = E_c - E_v$
Electrons must acquire at least this much energy to jump from the valence band to the conduction band.

3. Conductors, Semiconductors, and Insulators based on Band Theory

Conductor

The conduction band and valence band overlap. There is no forbidden gap ($E_g = 0$). Electrons are free to move even at 0K.

Semiconductor

There is a narrow forbidden gap ($E_g \approx 1 \text{ eV}$). At 0K it behaves as an insulator, but at room temperature, some electrons gain enough thermal energy to cross the gap.

Insulator

There is a wide forbidden gap ($E_g > 3 \text{ eV}$, typically ~5-9 eV). Electrons cannot cross the gap under normal conditions.

4. Doping Ratio

The standard doping ratio for an extrinsic semiconductor is usually 1 impurity atom per $10^6$ to $10^8$ semiconductor atoms.

Even this tiny ratio drastically increases the conductivity of the material by providing a massive number of free charge carriers.

5. Impurity Atoms in 1 Mole of Si

Given: 1 mole of Silicon atoms.
Number of atoms in 1 mole = Avogadro's Number = $6.022 \times 10^{23}$ atoms.

Assuming a standard doping ratio of 1 impurity atom for every $10^7$ silicon atoms ($1:10^7$):

Calculation:

$$ \text{Impurity atoms} = \frac{6.022 \times 10^{23}}{10^7} = 6.022 \times 10^{16} \text{ atoms} $$

6. Fermi Level ($E_f$) Positions

  • Intrinsic Semiconductor: The Fermi level lies exactly in the middle of the forbidden gap. ($E_f \approx \frac{E_c + E_v}{2}$)
  • N-Type Semiconductor: Due to excess electrons from donor impurities, the Fermi level shifts upwards, lying just below the conduction band ($E_c$).
  • P-Type Semiconductor: Due to excess holes from acceptor impurities, the Fermi level shifts downwards, lying just above the valence band ($E_v$).

7. Derivation of Einstein Equation (Relation)

The Einstein relation connects the diffusion constant ($D$) and mobility ($\mu$) of charge carriers. We derive this by considering a semiconductor at thermal equilibrium.

Step 1: Net current at equilibrium is zero.

Under non-uniform doping at thermal equilibrium, there is a built-in electric field $E$. The net current density for electrons ($J_n$) must be zero. Drift current equals and opposes diffusion current.

$$ J_n = J_{\text{drift}} + J_{\text{diff}} = 0 $$

$$ n q \mu_n E + q D_n \frac{dn}{dx} = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \mu_n E n = - D_n \frac{dn}{dx} $$

Step 2: Relate Electric Field to Potential.

Electric field $E = - \frac{dV}{dx}$, where $V$ is the electric potential.

$$ - \mu_n n \frac{dV}{dx} = - D_n \frac{dn}{dx} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{\mu_n}{D_n} dV = \frac{dn}{n} \quad \text{--- (Eq. 1)} $$

Step 3: Use Maxwell-Boltzmann Statistics.

The electron concentration $n$ in a potential field $V$ is given by classical thermodynamics (Boltzmann distribution):

$$ n = n_0 e^{\frac{qV}{kT}} $$

Differentiating both sides with respect to $V$:

$$ dn = n_0 \cdot \frac{q}{kT} e^{\frac{qV}{kT}} dV $$

Substitute $n$ back into the equation:

$$ dn = n \frac{q}{kT} dV \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{dn}{n} = \frac{q}{kT} dV \quad \text{--- (Eq. 2)} $$

Step 4: Equate Eq. 1 and Eq. 2.

$$ \frac{\mu_n}{D_n} dV = \frac{q}{kT} dV $$

$$ \frac{D_n}{\mu_n} = \frac{kT}{q} = V_T $$

Similarly, for holes: $\frac{D_p}{\mu_p} = \frac{kT}{q}$.
Here, $k$ is Boltzmann's constant, $T$ is absolute temperature, $q$ is charge, and $V_T$ is the thermal voltage (~26mV at room temp).

8. Hall Effect

Definition: When a current-carrying semiconductor (or conductor) is placed in a perpendicular magnetic field, a voltage is developed across the specimen in a direction perpendicular to both the current and the magnetic field. This phenomenon is called the Hall Effect.

Formulas

Hall Voltage: $$ V_H = \frac{B \cdot I \cdot R_H}{t} $$

Where $B$ = Magnetic field, $I$ = Current, $t$ = thickness.

Hall Coefficient ($R_H$): $$ R_H = \frac{1}{n q} \text{ (for electrons)} \quad \text{or} \quad \frac{1}{p q} \text{ (for holes)} $$

Physical Significance of $R_H$

  • Determines Carrier Type: If $R_H$ is negative, it's an N-type material (electrons). If positive, P-type (holes).
  • Determines Carrier Concentration: $n = \frac{1}{q R_H}$. Knowing $R_H$ allows us to find exactly how many charge carriers exist per unit volume.
  • Determines Mobility: Using conductivity $\sigma$, mobility $\mu = \sigma |R_H|$.

9. Standard Class Numericals (Important Types)

Note: These are the standard framework numericals generally covered in class for this topic.

Type 1: Mass Action Law ($np = n_i^2$)

Q: Pure Silicon has an intrinsic carrier concentration of $1.5 \times 10^{10} \text{ cm}^{-3}$. It is doped with Phosphorus to a concentration of $10^{16} \text{ cm}^{-3}$. Find the electron and hole concentrations.

Solution:
Phosphorus is a donor (N-type), so $N_D = 10^{16} \text{ cm}^{-3}$.
Majority carriers (electrons) $n \approx N_D = 10^{16} \text{ cm}^{-3}$.
Using Mass Action Law: $p = \frac{n_i^2}{n} = \frac{(1.5 \times 10^{10})^2}{10^{16}} = \frac{2.25 \times 10^{20}}{10^{16}} = 2.25 \times 10^4 \text{ cm}^{-3}$ (minority holes).

Type 2: Conductivity ($\sigma$)

Q: Calculate the conductivity of intrinsic Si at room temp. Given $n_i = 1.5 \times 10^{10} \text{ cm}^{-3}$, $\mu_n = 1300 \text{ cm}^2/\text{Vs}$, $\mu_p = 500 \text{ cm}^2/\text{Vs}$, $q = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$.

Solution:
$\sigma_i = n_i q (\mu_n + \mu_p)$
$\sigma_i = (1.5 \times 10^{10}) \times (1.6 \times 10^{-19}) \times (1300 + 500)$
$\sigma_i = 2.4 \times 10^{-9} \times 1800 = 4.32 \times 10^{-6} \text{ (\Omega cm)}^{-1}$

Type 3: Hall Effect

Q: An N-type Ge sample has a donor density of $10^{21} \text{ m}^{-3}$. Find its Hall coefficient.

Solution:
$n \approx N_D = 10^{21} \text{ m}^{-3}$.
$R_H = -\frac{1}{nq} = -\frac{1}{10^{21} \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19}} = -\frac{1}{160} = -6.25 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}^3/\text{C}$.

10. PN Junction Definitions

  • Space Charge (Region): Also known as the depletion region. It is the region near the PN junction interface stripped of free charge carriers (electrons and holes), leaving behind a 'space charge' of fixed, immobile positive (donor) and negative (acceptor) ions.
  • Depletion Region: The specific zone around the junction where mobile charge carriers have been depleted via recombination. It acts like an insulator blocking further spontaneous charge flow.
  • Barrier Potential ($V_B$): The electric potential difference established across the depletion region due to the exposed immobile ions. It opposes further diffusion of majority carriers across the junction. (~0.7V for Si, ~0.3V for Ge).
  • Knee Voltage (Cut-in/Threshold Voltage): The minimum forward bias voltage required to overcome the barrier potential and allow significant forward current to flow through the diode.

11. I-V Characteristics Graph (Forward & Reverse Bias)

Forward Bias (Quadrant I): Current is nearly zero until the applied voltage exceeds the Knee Voltage (0.7V for Si). After that, current increases exponentially with voltage.

Reverse Bias (Quadrant III): A very small, constant Reverse Saturation Current ($I_0$) flows due to minority carriers. If voltage is increased too much, Breakdown occurs (Zener or Avalanche), and current increases sharply.

12. Band Diagrams for PN Junction

The bending of energy bands occurs at the junction to align the Fermi levels (at equilibrium) or to reflect applied voltages.

1. Unbiased (Equilibrium)

Fermi level ($E_f$) is constant across the junction. Built-in potential barrier $qV_0$ prevents net flow.

2. Forward Biased

P-side is pushed DOWN (or N-side UP). Potential barrier is reduced to $q(V_0 - V_f)$. Electrons easily cross over.

3. Reverse Biased

P-side is pulled UP (or N-side DOWN). Potential barrier is increased to $q(V_0 + V_r)$. Flow is blocked.
